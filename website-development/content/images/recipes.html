01. Lamprais

Banana Leaf Bundles of Joy: Unwrapping Lamprais


The food scene of Sri Lanka is quite unique as it had influences from the British, Portuguese, Dutch and Malaysian. From 1658 until 1796 Sri Lanka was under occupation by the Netherlands, during this period a ethnic group known as the Dutch Burghers settled in Sri Lanka. The Dutch Burgher's were decendents from Dutch, Portuguese and Sri Lankan ancestry and they introduced a dish that would later become lamprais. The etymology of lamprais can be traced back to the Dutch word lomprijst or “parcel of food”. This is essentially Sri Lankan comfort food at its best! Each time I eat lamprais I can feel my face start to glow with happiness and my soul light up with excitement while my tummy puffs up like a balloon from all the accompaniments in the dish. The cool thing about lamprais is that the dish is flexible and varies in the ingredients used depending on the family. The side dishes used whenever my mum makes lamprais include Seeni Sambol (caramelised onion relish) which has a spicy and sweet taste, Wambotu Moju (eggplant pickle), and a type of short grain rice called Samba cooked in ghee, Fish Cutlets (croquettes with tuna and potato filling) and Chicken Curry all wrapped up and steamed in banana leaves. The inclusion of the banana imparts a unique aroma and taste to the dish, adding a subtle earthy note.

02. Kottu

Kottu: When Leftovers Become a Symphony of Flavour

Similar to Chinese fried rice that was invented to use leftover rice, Sri Lanka's kottu repurposes leftover Godamba roti (a popular local flatbread). This street food originated in the Tamil regions of Sri Lanka in the 1970s, likely in Trincomalee or Batticaloa. Throughout Sri Lanka, the distinctive sound of bench scrapers chopping and mixing roti with vegetables on metal cooktops is like music to the ears as a feast is about to arrive. As a Sri Lankan, I can attest that this flavourful, inexpensive dish is arguably one of Sri Lanka's hidden treasures, one I find myself often craving. The humble origins of this dish can serve as an important lesson for us all, the lesson that resourcefulness and transformation are skills we can use to transform something past it's prime into something extraodinary and delicious.
